    I just discovered that Apple's Pages can export documents into .epub (in that sense it's a similar suggestion to Scrivener, which I'll have to try out next). I've tested the Pages epub export feature with text documents that included images, tables and footnotes - all were converted into epub without issues (i.e. I confirmed that it looked and worked as expected on an iPhone, with the Adobe Digital Editions app and that the files could be opened with Calbre and Sigil).

    [Update] I see that there is an excellent discussion of the pros and cons of exporting epub from Pages and Scrivener on the ThreePress Blog.

    On a related note, if you're specifically targeting the iphone/ipad I know that there was a session at WWDC 2011 that described best practices for how to create both flowing text and fixed layout (epub based) ebooks (including features like embedded HTML5 video and audio) for these devices. Unfortunately the content of the video & the slides for the session are still under non-disclosure. Check back in a few weeks if you're not a registered Apple developer.

  3. Kevin Hawkins
    Member

    On Miriam's original question on this thread, if you aren't in a position to start with webpages or TEI and want to be able to work with things more visually, see Wikipedia's list of free desktop publishing software. Some of these may be able to produce EPUB files as InDesign can, and they may let you embed multimedia content.

    The easy-to-use tool for creating multimedia ebooks is iBooks Author, but your content is then locked into Apple's proprietary flavor of EPUB or in PDF and can only be read on an iPad. See our guide to iBooks Author.

    As for Pedram's recent question on this thread, there is no simple answer here. Answers to a few questions will help guide you in the right direction:

    a) Do you need for this book to be sold as an e-book on Amazon, in the iBook Store, or in another retail outlet? Or are you content to put files on your own website and let people download from there?

    b) Do you need for a user to be able to read the book on an e-reader, or is it okay to require them to have a web browser?

  5. Kevin Hawkins
    Member

    Amazon sells books in print and in formats that can be read by Amazon Kindle devices. Sound and film obviously cannot be in a print book, and I believe that the Kindle formats do not yet support these either. (On the other hand, iBooks Author allows you to create ebooks with these types of media, but then they can be used on Apple, not Amazon, devices.)

    So you will need to determine whether you are willing to omit some of your content in the version sold on Amazon in order to reach the audience found there.

    An update to my own earlier comment and a partial reply to Wally Grotophorst's: I have since had cause to use Sigil to correct the imperfect epub export produced by current Calibre. I would very much recommend Sigil over Calibre for epub2 at this point. (The document in question was a "reprint" collection of stories, poems, and review essays for a webzine, and thus with some visual complications--indented lines of verse--as well as structural requirements analogous to a chaptered book's.)
