  3. St√©fan Sinclair
    Admin

    Eric, I certainly share your interest in historical perspectives on humanities computing. Beyond fairly discipline-specific retrospectives oriented toward published research (the 1991 anniversary addition of CHUM contains several of these), I don't know of much along the lines of what you describe. I sometimes daydream of doing some video interviews with some of the pioneers still with us (Raben, Burrows, Hockey, etc.), but then the realities of my current commitments rudely shake me awake :). Still, I think this is fairly urgent, and it may not even be that time consuming to at least do the interviews, even if the synthetic work is delayed until later. But it's probably best for a historian or ethnographer to lead the work, rather than someone like me with a literary background :).

    Another person to talk to about this is Edward Vanhoutte who has compiled a lot of materials on humanities computing. I know Willard McCarty has also digitized a ton of early stuff, and Geoffrey Rockwell has considered more closely some of the early Canadian work.

    I certainly hope that someone takes this up as a project!

  5. aearhart
    Member

    Bethany is correct. I'm working on a book length project on digital literary studies. It started out as a broader DH book, but that seemed far too general. I'm tracing the development through forms--edition, archive--and approaches--content selection and fragmented data analysis like visualization, data mining, mapping, etc.

    I do think it is important that we, as a field, begin to collect and analyze our early history. I've had great conversations with pioneers of the field and still have a number of folks on the list to contact (unsworth!). I'm hoping that this project will be complete within the next two years.

    Glad to hear that folks are interested in the questions that the project poses. You never know....
