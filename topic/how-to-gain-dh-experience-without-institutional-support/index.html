    That is indeed a bit of tricky situation, not least because some of the usual strategies for building momentum and community around a project (like travelling to do conference presentations) may not be viable options. If you're comfortable letting us know your general geographical location, there may be some connections folks here could help establish.

  5. Patrick Murray-John

    I think the most interesting part is in this phrase from your question: "how do I obtain CV-level involvement (for lack of a better term) in DH projects".

    What is "CV-level" in DH is a huge, open question, one that DH struggles with all over. It hits on what is accepted as scholarship?, what counts toward tenure? alt-ac careers, and many other questions!

    So, CV-level? depends on the CV, and what jobs you are applying for. Last time I was job hunting, I had a couple different CVs for different position types. One listed unconferences that I'd attended, and (if applicable) what sessions I'd led. That was in a section under "Conferences". I think I also had an "Ongoing Projects" or "Development" or some-such thing, where I gave short descriptions and links to stuff I was building or had built.

    That works (depending on the CV/job interaction), but doesn't get to the "plugging away on your own" aspect. I'm all behind the idea of sending an email out, and looking for local DH/hacker communities.

    Going that route, I'd also suggest posting your dataset on GitHub, and using whatever networks you can to invite playing and working with that data. If there's a (DH) hackathon you can find in your area that invites data submissions (like GLAMHackPhilly did recently), that's a great place to make contacts, build up the project, and list the hackathon attendance and submission on your DH-oriented CV -- those non-standard CV lines, and your boldness in using them, will likely be recognized by DHers.

  7. Patrick Murray-John

    Replying to @trmarvin's post:

    That is one of the sticky wickets! No matter what, it would be a bold move, and as such an implicit positioning of yourself in the market. That could obviously go either way, but since there are wide parts of DH that are all about challenging traditional publishing model, it would serve to stake out a place among DHers, and possibly establish DH cred. But yeah, I understand/remember how bold a move that would be!

    To the details of authorship, I'll skip the copyrightability question of data by assuming it falls into the category of data that reflects significant creative work (as opposed to, say, the phone book). So, your copyright would already be implicitly there by publishing it (I think...we're moving into 'I am not a lawyer' territory!). To make it more solid, researching what license to apply to it would be the next step. Creative Commons's page about data might be a start.

    That wouldn't prevent someone from downloading the data, but presumably you have a head start on working with it for your research questions. If someone were to abuse the license, you'd still have to react to that, but you'd have the tools in place. But, I tend to be optimistic about scholarly integrity in general, and figure that if someone else does publish some results based on your data, they'll include the citation (maybe even reach out for a collaboration, as DH folks tend to do!). Ideally in that situation, they'll produce results/arguments that don't nullify yours. That actually seems likely to me, given the very different directions, methodologies, etc. that can be used on some data.

    So, it is tricky and a little risky. But, if the mission is to have CV lines that say "Digital Humanist", doing the research to decide on a license and posting the data up would put you deep in some of the important conversations going on in DH, just by adding a CV line that's a link to the dataset.

    Hope that helps

  10. lmullen

    I'll add a second to Patrick's ideas about making data available. Putting the data out there marks you as the owner, both in a copyright sense but more importantly in a scholarly sense. It announces that that particular topic is one that you are working on.

    But it doesn't make that announcement in a "keep your hands off" sort of way. Putting something valuable (i.e., your data) out there means that potential collaborators have a reason to collaborate you. At least my own experience with making data available is that it gives other scholars something to use, and therefore a reason to contribute their own data back to the project.

    Along those lines, you might consider making a simple web page for your project. If you are using GitHub anyway, then GitHub pages might be worth checking out. A simple web page might not be a digital project itself, but it can go a long way towards finding people to work with.

    What this all adds up to is that if you're in a place without institutional support and without existing connections to DH scholars, you may as well start working on your project and making it public, which can be your means of entrance into the DH community and DH collaboration. Trevor Owens writes that "There is a version of whatever it is that you want to do that you can do right now with only an investment of your time and energy." Trevor's whole post might be of interest to you.
