  2. Amanda Visconti
    Key Master

    The University of Maryland's Professor Matt Kirschenbaum has written a blog post on this topic that might be a good starting place for you: http://otal.umd.edu/~mgk/blog/archives/000869.html

    Although you'll want to do more research to turn up newer programs (the post was written five years ago), Matt makes a good point: when applying to programs, think about where you see yourself going. Are you interested in working in a more traditional humanities community but with digital tools? Do you want to create electronic art or work in a DH center? Does a program, digital or not, have faculty who are willing to mentor the kind of work you'd like to do?

    Try Googling some of the synonyms for DH such as humanities computing, digital studies, etc. I found my current program by researching university DH centers, then looking into the programs associated with the centers. I noticed that good DH centers were almost always linked with at least one strong academic department, but that those strong departments weren't always in my particular area of interest. If you're attracted to a school on the grounds of the work you see coming from their DH center, look at which departments share a strong linkage with the center (e.g. shared faculty, workshop sponsorship).

    Replying to @Elizabeth Glascock's post:

    In addition to looking at masters programs that are labeled "digital humanities," I'd recommend that you look at masters programs in the iSchools (www.ischools.org), which are mostly (but not all) schools with masters in library and information science. DH fits well into many of these programs and is represented among their faculty. At Illinois (wwww.lis.illinois.edu), we have a number of courses and faculty members relevant to this interest, and we deliver the degree both online (in a hybrid, partly synchronous format) and on campus...
