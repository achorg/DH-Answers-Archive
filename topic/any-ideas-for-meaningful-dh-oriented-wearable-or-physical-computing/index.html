    I'm interested in wearables and physical computing -- in the sense of tactile, embodied interaction like that made possible by the Lilypad Arduino, a sew-able, programmable microprocessor. My problem is that, conceptually, I can't get past toys and art pieces -- like blinky, light-up merit badges, ambient orbs that respond to the state of my inbox, a tipsyflower made of shape-memory wire & breathalyzer parts, jewelry that whines when you walk north...

    I'd like to design a project that I felt was meaningful in terms of digital humanities theory or research -- something useful within, or reflective about, the field. And I'm just stuck. Any ideas?

    No experience in soft circuits or arduinos necessary: I've just got a weird kind of writer's block and am looking for inspiration.

    Last year we had a group of students in humanities computing (MA) and in design (MDes) in a reading course on interactives. Most of them did a final project together which involved building on the Monome -> Arduinome. They basically developed a basic platform for experimenting with interactivity built on the whole Arduinome design and then illustrated a number of different things that could be done with this platform. I was sufficiently impressed that I have commissioned two of the boxes (one nice touch - they designed an enclosure for the platform) so that we can begin a series of experiments in public interactivity.

    Another student in the reading course created a jacket that could be used as a prop for magic shows. He is a performing magician - his jacket has lights under the fabric that he could trigger by playing with a jacket button. The light sequences were designed to add to his performances.

    I don't know if these ideas are useful, and I would love to have you involved in the interactivity project.

    One thing we did find in the course was that:

    a) there was a real difference between how MA students do research and how MDes students do it.

    b) the design students are used to iteratively sketching ideas in a way that got the students unstuck.

    So, I guess I'm recommending that you talk to some design folk about how they think about design. A book I like that describes some design practices is Brenda Laurel's collection on _Design Research_.
