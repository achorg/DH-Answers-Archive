Sometimes I'd think I'd done a great job explaining, only to have librarians tell me later that my explanation was confusing. I've learned to try to tailor my explanation to the group I'm talking to, and to spend a lot of time on a few projects rather than slaloming through a bunch. So if I'm in a presentation setting, I'll try to get a sense of what the audience might find interesting. Some of my go-to example projects are those that are easily grasped, visually impressive, and clearly accomplish something that couldn't easily be accomplished as a monograph: <a href="https://republicofletters.stanford.edu/">Mapping the Republic of Letters, </a><a href="http://www.americanpast.org/voting/">Voting America</a>, and the <a href="http://www.slavevoyages.org/tast/index.faces">Trans-Atlantic Slave Trade Database</a>, for example. You don't need a technical background to see what these projects do and why they're cool. </p> <p>My favorite online directories include the <a href="http://commons.gc.cuny.edu/wiki/index.php/The_CUNY_Digital_Humanities_Resource_Guide">CUNY Digital Humanities Guide</a>, Lisa Spiro's <a href="http://digitalscholarship.wordpress.com/2011/10/14/getting-started-in-the-digital-humanities/">"Getting Started in the Digital Humanities,"</a> and <a href="http://arts-humanities.net/">arts-humanities.net</a>.</p> <p>There are many examples of library-based DH, too: <a href="http://scholar.library.miami.edu/archivoteatral/">The Cuban Theater Digital Archive</a>, the <a href="http://livingstone.library.ucla.edu/">Livingstone Spectral Imaging Project</a>, and the <a href="http://docsouth.unc.edu/blueridgeparkway/">Digital Blue Ridge Parkway</a>, to name a few.
