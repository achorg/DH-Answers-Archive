They're tremendous regional networking opportunities as well -- but you'll also find a welcoming community at the annual <a href="http://digitalhumanities.org">DH conference</a> (where the <a href="http://ach.org">ACH</a> hosts a longstanding <a href="http://ach.org/mentoring">mentoring program</a>, which now runs year-round) and at conferences like SDH-SEMI (see, for example, <a href="http://sdh-semi.org/2012/04/01/introductory-workshopts-on-digital-humanities-at-congress-2012-in-waterloo/">these workshops</a>, held just last week).</p> <p>Most of the North American training institutes with which I'm familiar (like <a href="http://dhsi.org">DHSI</a> and the new <a href="http://mith.umd.edu/dhwi/">DHWI</a>) are open to independent scholars and work hard to cover costs or keep them reasonably low. The various NEH-supported <a href="http://www.neh.gov/divisions/odh/institutes">IATDH programs</a> are generally a bit more advanced (that's what the "A" stands for), but also open to people without institutional affiliation and costs are usually covered. </p> <p>More and more training programs are being attached to disciplinary conferences. I'm not sure what's in the works for AHA, but the <a href="http://dhcommons.org/">DH Commons</a> group will host another "Getting Started in DH" workshop at the MLA conference this January. (And you should definitely register with and explore DH Commons in general!)</p> <p>Staying plugged in with Twitter is now a must. <a href="http://digitalhumanitiesnow.org/">DH Now</a> can help you follow the conversations being had in the community, and discover new resources. Sometimes I'd think I'd done a great job explaining, only to have librarians tell me later that my explanation was confusing. I've learned to try to tailor my explanation to the group I'm talking to, and to spend a lot of time on a few projects rather than slaloming through a bunch. So if I'm in a presentation setting, I'll try to get a sense of what the audience might find interesting. Some of my go-to example projects are those that are easily grasped, visually impressive, and clearly accomplish something that couldn't easily be accomplished as a monograph: <a href="https://republicofletters.stanford.edu/">Mapping the Republic of Letters, </a><a href="http://www.americanpast.org/voting/">Voting America</a>, and the <a href="http://www.slavevoyages.org/tast/index.faces">Trans-Atlantic Slave Trade Database</a>, for example.
