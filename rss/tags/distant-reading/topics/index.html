Let me just dump a handful of links which I think might be helpful from my recent delicious/pinboard (hmm... delicious pinboard).</p> <p>While I can't point to specific results/papers. Aditi Muralidharan (@silverasm)'s WordSeer project is promising interesting results about slave narratives, though it does not rely on TEI. In <a href="http://www.eecs.berkeley.edu/~aditi/projects/wordseer.html">this talk</a>, though, she does a good job of answering Sean's question directly--how can metadata improve on generic full-text search. WordSeer (as I understand it) works mostly by supplementing raw text with part of speech tagging and other grammatical metadata.</p> <p>With respect to TEI specifically one obvious place to start, I think, is <a href="http://www.monkproject.org/">MONK</a>; as Dorothea notes, Martin Mueller is involved with the MONK project. You can find him summarizing MONK <a href="http://www.cch.kcl.ac.uk/cocoon/tei2008/programme/abstracts/abstract-169.html">here</a> and at greater length <a href="https://apps.lis.illinois.edu/wiki/display/MONK/Notes+towards+a+user+manual+of+Monk">here</a>. Unlike WordSeer, the Monk Workbench gives you metadata about author (including gender) and publication date. Matt Kirschenbaum mentions MONK in <a href="http://www.cs.umbc.edu/~hillol/NGDM07/abstracts/talks/MKirschenbaum.pdf">this article</a> [PDF].</p> <p>(How relevant it will be to you, I don't know, but when I heard John Unsworth speak recently, he talked about the work flow and complications of large text collections with different encoding standards--with reference to MONK in particular. You can hear him <a href="http://www.scholarslab.org/podcasts/unsworth-idiosyncrasy-at-scale/">here</a>.)</p> <p>There was a <a href="http://listserv.brown.edu/archives/cgi-bin/wa?A1=ind1104&amp;L=TEI-L&amp;F=&amp;S=&amp;O=T&amp;H=0&amp;D=0&amp;T=1#34">recent discussion</a> on the TEI list about eXist from which I learned a lot--again, about what's possible rather than specific results/reports.</p> <p>Finally--this one is also not TEI specific--if you haven't seen Ben Schmidt's blog, he is doing some pretty impressive stuff. This post, on <a href="http://sappingattention.blogspot.com/2011/04/age-cohort-and-vocabulary-use.html">vocabulary use and age cohort</a> gives some idea. </p>
