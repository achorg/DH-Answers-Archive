I'm comparing the relationships between scientific theories, artistic representations, and quantitative/tractable models of research objects (in any field) that make them computationally addressable. </p> <p>The question is this: which essays or articles would best introduce these advanced undergraduates to the third category? </p> <p>Willard McCarty's essay, "Modeling: a study in words and meanings" from *A companion to digital humanities* (2004), is cited in Matt Burton's "Joy of Topic Modeling" blog post last month &lt; <a href="http://mcburton.net/blog/joy-of-tm/" rel="nofollow">http://mcburton.net/blog/joy-of-tm/</a> &gt;. This seems the natural place to start, but I wonder if readers have used other texts to induce intelligent non-specialists to join, or at least to grasp, our field.</p> <p>These are my other texts:<br /> * David Bohm, *On Creativity*<br /> * Virginia Woolf, *The Waves* (and Stephen Ramsay's work on the same)<br /> * Richard Dawkins, *The Oxford Book of Modern Science Writing*</p> <p>With thanks,<br /> Michael Ullyot </p>
