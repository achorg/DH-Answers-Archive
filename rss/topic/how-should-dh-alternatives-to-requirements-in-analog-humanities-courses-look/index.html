In other words, within the scope of a seminar I'd not do twice the work. Would I use the opportunity of a paper to work through the potential theoretical challenges of a digital project, and do what I could to plan the project I'd eventually do? Sure. But would I also do the project? Not if I was teaching and had two other seminars, no.</p> <p>I'm also of the mind that a professor who does not also&mdash;from day one&mdash;offer a digital alternative clearly defined is not a professor able to adequately judge the intellectual, creative, and technical merits of a digital project. And that's great, actually, to my mind, as it reduces the risk that professors will stamp simple static websites with suspect design as spectacular contributions to the field when they're just, shall we say, <em>not</em>.</p> <p>Some might argue that it's our duty to push forth and make professors change their requirements, and I'm all for education of someone already willing to learn/who has an open mind. But if that professor isn't truly able to give the same level of feedback, support, and mentoring that they would with the analog assignments...I just don't know.
