We should note that Anglo-Saxonists were among the *very* early adopters of DH, back in the late 80s-early 90s, and long before that, if you think of the digitization of the Dictionary of OE corpus starting in the 70s! (DOE website here: <a href="http://www.doe.utoronto.ca/)" rel="nofollow">http://www.doe.utoronto.ca/)</a> My sense is that now, there is some cool and important digital scholarship going on in the field, but much of it involves texts and manuscripts not of direct interest to beginners in the language – Latin sources, commentaries, etc. The Boethius Project <a href="http://beowulf.engl.uky.edu/~kiernan/eBoethius/inlad.htm" rel="nofollow">http://beowulf.engl.uky.edu/~kiernan/eBoethius/inlad.htm</a> and here <a href="http://www.english.ox.ac.uk/boethius/" rel="nofollow">http://www.english.ox.ac.uk/boethius/</a> is a great example. The only thing that I might add is that there are <a href="http://ang.wikipedia.org/wiki/N%C4%ABwu_Englisc_spr%C7%A3c">some pages in Wikipedia in Old English</a>. As beginning reading exercises, those might be a place to start, at least to get used to some of the morphology. Clearly, much of the vocabulary won't overlap much with poetry, though.</p> <p>A non-digital resource that I'd suggest is Barney's <a href="http://www.amazon.com/Word-Hoard-Introduction-English-Vocabulary-Language/dp/0300035063/ref=sr_1_1?ie=UTF8&amp;qid=1286980940&amp;sr=8-1">Word Hoard</a>. It's a nifty summary of the etymology and background of some of the most common words in OE. The really helpful thing about it, besides giving super-interesting etymology and relationships to other words in various languages, is that after browsing through lots of the entries it starts to help you see the patterns of how OE words became Modern English words (things like "stān" -&gt; "stone")</p> <p>Good luck to your friend! </p> Carin Ruff on "Online Teaching Resources for Old English?" http://digitalhumanities.org/answers/topic/online-teaching-resources-for-old-english#post-412 Wed, 13 Oct 2010 07:17:46 +0000 Carin Ruff 412@http://digitalhumanities.org/answers/ <p><em>Replying to @<a href='/DH-Answers-Archive/profile/melissaterras'>MelissaTerras</a>'s <a href="http://digitalhumanities.org/answers/topic/online-teaching-resources-for-old-english#post-409">post</a>:</em></p> <p>Peter Baker's intro really is the way to go.
