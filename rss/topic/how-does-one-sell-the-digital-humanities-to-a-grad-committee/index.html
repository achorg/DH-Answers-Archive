McGann, among others, but I can speak directly to the consequences of "doing" DH as an integral element of my dissertation work.</p> <p>The dissertation itself was conceived as a critical intervention in the emerging discourse around hypertext and electronic text more generally. The DH component of it was primarily in the realm of scholarly communication and information design, by means of writing the dissertation openly, in public, on the Web (1.0), in straight HTML. In essence, I was working toward the kind of open access scholarship we now see with resources like MediaCommons, CommentPress, Twitter, and so forth without any of that social media apparatus being in place (the dates here are roughly 1996-9). Nonetheless, even in this early rendition, the network turned out to be an excellent place to, well . . . network. Readers found my work online, including some of the more prominent critics and commentators in the field, which in turn led to my work getting linked to and cited, invitations to conferences, and so forth--that kind of recognition turned into the kind of near-term gratification that is so rare when one is embedded within the longue duree of scholarly dissertation writing.</p> <p>I think the key take-away here is that whatever the specific form your digital humanities work takes, the salient argument to your committee has to be about scholarly communication and professionalization. On these terms the significance of DH is unassailable. </p> <p>And may I offer one other piece of advice? Think of DH less as a "thing," that is a bill of goods to be sold, and more as a kind of ethos, or commitment to the way your want to do your work, long-term, in whatever venue or circumstances you find yourself. Otherwise the risk is one of the "digital" becoming a kind of add-on, something that you can reward yourself with once you've done the "foundational" work of the dissertation. But tools, platforms, and networks are pretty foundational in and of themselves, or ought to be, or so it seems to me.</p> <p>Good luck! </p> Dorothea Salo on "How does one 'sell' the Digital Humanities to a grad committee?" http://digitalhumanities.org/answers/topic/how-does-one-sell-the-digital-humanities-to-a-grad-committee#post-1184 Mon, 23 May 2011 18:22:04 +0000 Dorothea Salo 1184@http://digitalhumanities.org/answers/ <p>Steve Hitchcock maintains <a href="http://opcit.eprints.org/oacitation-biblio.html">an entire webliography</a> on the open-access citation-advantage question.
